Origin Story

From One Conversation to a System

It started with a simple question

A classmate came to me for advice about graduate school. Instead of giving recommendations, I asked: "Why do you want to pursue a graduate degree?"

Core Finding

Anxiety rarely stems from lack of ability. It comes from flawed mental models‚Äîmisunderstanding how GPA works, overestimating the permanence of early choices, confusing fear with information.

Pattern: False Structure

Most student anxiety is built on wrong premises‚Äîarithmetic errors in GPA calculation, information gaps about policy, over-modeling an uncertain future. The structure feels real but isn't.

Intervention Mode

The first step isn't giving answers‚Äîit's dismantling wrong frameworks. "Path grows, not chosen." "Choice ‚â† abandonment; choice = sequencing."

Representative Cases (Anonymized)

Case A: The Calculator Error

A student believed her GPA was "locked in" and unrecoverable after freshman year. Actual calculation showed she was only 0.15 points behind her goal‚Äîachievable in one semester. Months of anxiety from a mathematical misconception.

Case B: The False Lock-In

A high-anxiety student wanted to "lock in a safe path" to eliminate uncertainty. Interview revealed: the paths he feared closing were already open, and the "safe" path he wanted didn't actually provide safety.

Case C: The Narrative Gap

A student with strong thinking ability appeared "directionless." Issue wasn't capability but narrative ability‚Äîcouldn't integrate scattered insights into a coherent story about herself.

Connection to MindForge

These interviews directly informed MindForge's design principle: articulate values before exploring options. Users who skip self-reflection chase external validation instead of internal purpose. The three-phase dialogue structure exists because Meal Chatting Hours revealed that jumping to "what should I do?" fails when users haven't answered "what do I actually want?"
